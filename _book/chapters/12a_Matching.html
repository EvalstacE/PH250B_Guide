8  Introduction to Matching

1. Introduction to Matching

What is Matching?

Matching is the selection of a reference series (e.g., controls in a case-control study or unexposed in a cohort study) that is nearly identical to the index series with respect to one or more potential confounders.

  • Goal: To improve the quality of the counterfactual by making study groups more comparable.

Why Match?

Beyond reducing confounding, matching is used to:

  1. Increase Statistical Efficiency (Precision): Prevents situations where confounder distributions are dramatically different between groups (e.g., all cases are old, all controls are young).

  2. Increase Statistical Power: Particularly in small case-control studies, ensuring sufficient controls in important subgroups.

  3. Feasibility: Practical reasons during enrollment (e.g., selecting the “next birth” as a control in a hospital study).

Types of Matching

  • Individual (Pair) Matching: Matching specific subjects based on characteristics (e.g., for every 25-year-old male case, find a 25-year-old male control).

  • Frequency Matching: Matching the distribution of characteristics (e.g., if 30% of cases are women, ensure 30% of controls are women).

  • Distance Matching: Using algorithms (like propensity scores) to find the “closest” match based on multiple variables in multidimensional space.

Matching in Different Study Designs

Study Design Method Purpose/Effect
Cohort Match Exposed to Unexposed Removes confounding in the design phase. Analysis does not require special adjustment for the matched factor.
Case-Control Match Cases to Controls Does NOT remove confounding (can introduce selection bias). Must use a matched analysis (stratification) to fix this. Primarily done to improve efficiency.
Trials Block Randomization Matches participants by block (e.g., geography) before randomizing. Increases efficiency.

Disadvantages of Matching

  • Cost/Complexity: Can make sampling difficult.

  • Exclusion: If no match is found for a case, that case might be excluded (reduces N).

  • Reduced Flexibility: You cannot estimate the association of the matched variable with the outcome (e.g., if you match on age, you can’t study age as a risk factor).

  • Overmatching: Improper matching can obscure the effect of interest (discussed in future lectures).

2. Numerical Examples of Matching

A. Matching in a Cohort Study

In a cohort study, matching manipulates the exposure distribution .

  • Scenario: A crude analysis of a population shows a Risk Ratio (RR) of 33 due to strong confounding by gender (90% of exposed are men, 10% of unexposed are men).

  • The Match: We design the study to select unexposed subjects such that their gender distribution matches the exposed group (e.g., taking a 10% sample of exposed men and ensuring the unexposed sample is also 90% male).

  • Result: The new Crude RR becomes 10, which matches the stratum-specific RRs.

  • Takeaway: Matching on a confounder in a cohort study removes confounding. You do not need to adjust for the matched variable in the analysis.

B. Matching in a Case-Control Study

In a case-control study, matching manipulates the disease (case/control) distribution.

  • Scenario: We match controls to cases based on gender.

  • The Problem: By forcing the controls to look like the cases regarding gender (a confounder associated with exposure), we inadvertently make the controls look more like the cases regarding the exposure as well. This biases the crude association toward the null.

  • Result: A crude analysis of the matched data yields an Odds Ratio (OR) of 5.0, which is biased (lower than the true OR of 10).

  • The Fix: You must stratify (perform a matched analysis) to obtain the correct estimate. When stratified by the matching factor, the OR returns to 10.

  • Takeaway: Matching in a case-control study does not remove confounding; it actually introduces a selection bias that must be corrected by stratifying on the matched variable in the analysis.

“Breaking the Match”

  • Cohort Studies: If you ignore the matching (break the match) in the analysis, the point estimate remains valid, though variance estimates (CIs) may be incorrect.

  • Case-Control Studies: If you ignore the matching in the analysis, you will produce a biased estimate. You generally cannot break the match in case-control studies.
