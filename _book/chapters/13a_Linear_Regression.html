12  Linear Regression

When to Use Linear Regression

You typically choose a linear regression model based on the type of outcome (dependent) variable you have:

  • Outcome Type: Continuous (e.g., height-for-age Z-score, blood pressure, weight).

  • Measure of Association: Mean Difference

  • Identity Function: Identity Link (we model the outcome directly, without transformation).

Important Note: While it is mathematically possible to model risks or rates with linear regression, it is generally avoided because the model can predict impossible values (e.g., risk < 0 or > 1)

The Bivariable Linear Model:

\[E(Y|X=x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1x\]

  • \(E(Y|X=x)\): The expected mean of the outcome \(Y\) given exposure \(X\)

  • \(\beta_0\) (Intercept): The mean of the outcome \(Y\) when the exposure \(X = 0\) (the unexposed/reference group)

  • \(\beta_1\) (Slope): The Mean Difference in the outcome \(Y\) for a one-unit change in \(X\)

Interpretting the Coefficients:

Scenario A: Binary Exposure (e.g., Treatment vs. Control)

If \(X\) is binary (\(0 = \text{Unexposed}\), \(1 = \text{Exposed}\)):

  • \(\beta_0\): The mean outcome among the unexposed

  • \(\beta_1\): The difference in the mean outcome between the exposed (\(X=1\)) and unexposed (\(X=0\))

Scenario B: Continuous Exposure (e.g., Weight in kg)

If \(X\) is continuous:

  • \(\beta_1\): The change in the mean outcome for a one-unit increase in \(X\)

NOTE: Sometimes a “one-unit increase” (e.g., 1 gram of weight) is too small to be meaningful.

  • You may need to calculate the effect of a larger shift (e.g., 10 units or 1 Standard Deviation).

  • To do this, simply multiply the coefficient by the amount of the shift (e.g., \(\beta_1 \times 10\))

Calculating the Mean Difference (the “Plug-In” Method)

You should be able to derive the mean difference using the coefficients. The professors emphasize “plugging in” values to prove the interpretation.

Step-by-Step Calculation:

To find the Mean Difference comparing Exposed (\(X=1\)) to Unexposed (\(X=0\)):

  1. Write the Model: \(E(Y|X) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X\)

  2. Calculate Mean for Exposed (\(X=1\)):

* $$\beta_0 + \beta_1(1) = \beta_0 + \beta_1$$
  1. Calculate Mean for Unexposed (\(X=0\))
* $$\beta_0 + \beta_1(0) = \beta_0$$
  1. Subtract (Exposed - Unexposed):
* $$(\beta_0 + \beta_1) - (\beta_0) = \beta_1$$

RESULT: The Mean Difference is equal to \(\beta_1\)

Multivariable Linear Regression (Adjusting for Confounding)

When we add more variables (covariates) to adjust for confounding, the interpretation changes slightly.

\[E(Y|X, Z) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \beta_2 Z\]

(Where \(X\) is exposure and \(Z\) is a confounder)

  • \(\beta_1\): The difference in the mean outcome comparing exposed vs. unexposed, holding \(Z\) constant (or adjusting for \(Z\))

  • \(\beta_2\): The difference in the mean outcome comparing levels of \(Z\), holding exposure \(X\) constant

Interaction in Linear Models

If we suspect interaction (effect modification), we add a product term (interaction term; measure of effect modification) to the model.

\[E(Y|X) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \beta_3 (X_1 \cdot X_2)\]

\(\beta_3\) (Interaction Term): This coefficient assesses the presence of interaction

Interpretation: \(\beta_3\) represents the difference between the observed joint effect of \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) and the expected joint effect (sum of individual effects)

Scale: Linear regression models assess interaction on the Additive Scale

Assessing Interaction:

  • If \(\beta_3 > 0\): Positive interaction (synergy)

  • If \(\beta_3 < 0\): Negative interaction (antagonism)

  • If \(\beta_3 = 0\): No interaction

Key Take-Aways:

Independence: The model assumes data are independent. It assumes NO clustering (e.g., people within households) and NO auto-correlation (repeated measures on the same person over time).

  • If clustering exists: You must use Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) or Mixed Models

Linearity: The model assumes a straight-line relationship between the independent and dependent variables. It assumes a 1-unit increase in X has the same effect on Y regardless of the starting value of X

Extrapolation: Be cautious when interpreting results outside the range of observed data (e.g., predicting height for a weight of 0 kg)
