18  Effect Modification, the Potential Outcomes Model, and Causal Interaction

Overview: The “Punchline”

Why do epidemiologists often argue that the Additive Scale is the appropriate scale for detecting causal (biological) interaction?

  • Using the Potential Outcomes (Counterfactual) framework, we can demonstrate that departures from additivity (where Observed Joint Effect \(\neq\) Expected Joint Effect) imply the presence of causal interactive types in the population.

  • Therefore, finding interaction on the additive scale suggests a true biological or causal interaction between two exposures.

  • In contrast, interaction on the relative (multiplicative) scale does not necessarily imply causal interaction.

1. Counterfactual “Types” (Recap: One Exposure)

Recall that for a single binary exposure, there are 4 potential “types” of individuals in a population:

  1. Doomed (Type 1): Gets disease regardless of exposure (\(Y_{a=1}=1, Y_{a=0}=1\)).

  2. Causal (Type 2): Disease caused by exposure (\(Y_{a=1}=1, Y_{a=0}=0\)).

  3. Preventive (Type 3): Exposure prevents disease (\(Y_{a=1}=0, Y_{a=0}=1\)).

  4. Immune (Type 4): Never gets disease (\(Y_{a=1}=0, Y_{a=0}=0\)).

  • Risk Ratio (RR): Depends on “Doomed” types (\(p_1\)) and “Causal” types (\(p_2\)).

  • Risk Difference (RD): Only depends on “Causal” (\(p_2\)) and “Preventive” (\(p_3\)) types (those amenable to intervention).

2. Counterfactual Types with Two Exposures

When we consider two exposures (X and Z), the number of potential types expands to 16.

  • Each type represents a specific pattern of potential outcomes under the 4 possible exposure combinations (\(XZ\): 11, 10, 01, 00).

  • 10 of these types reflect Causal Interaction (the outcome depends on both exposures).

  • 6 of these types reflect No Causal Interaction (at least one factor has no effect, or the effect is constant).

Examples of Interaction Types

  • Type 8 (Synergism): Gets disease only when both X and Z are present (\(Y_{11}=1\), all others=0).

  • Type 5 (Antagonism/Blocking): Gets disease unless X is absent and Z is present (specific combination blocks disease).

3. The Logic: Why Additive Interaction Implies Causal Types

We can prove this by calculating the Risk Differences (RD) assuming NO interacting types exist in the population. If our observed data contradicts this (i.e., additivity fails), then interacting types must exist.

Step A: Calculate Risks assuming NO Interaction Types

We sum the probabilities (\(p\)) of the non-interacting types (Types 1, 4, 6, 11, 13, 16) that result in disease for each exposure scenario:

  • \(R_{11}\) (Both present): \(p_1 + p_4 + p_6\)

  • \(R_{01}\) (Z only): \(p_1 + p_4 + p_{11}\)

  • \(R_{10}\) (X only): \(p_1 + p_6 + p_{13}\)

  • \(R_{00}\) (Neither): \(p_1 + p_{11} + p_{13}\)

Step B: Calculate Indvidual Risk Differences

  • RD for Z alone (\(RD_{01}\)): \(R_{01} - R_{00} = p_4 - p_{13}\)

  • RD for X alone (\(RD_{10}\)): \(R_{10} - R_{00} = p_6 - p_{11}\)

Step C: Calculate Joint Risk Difference

  • RD for Both (\(RD_{11}\)): \(R_{11} - R_{00} = (p_4 + p_6) - (p_{11} + p_{13})\)

Step D: The Additivity Check

If we sum the individual effects (\(RD_{01} + RD_{10}\)):

\[ (p_4 - p_{13}) + (p_6 - p_{11}) = p_4 + p_6 - p_{11} - p_{13} \]

This matches the formula for the Joint RD (\(RD_{11}\)) exactly!

\[ RD_{11} = RD_{01} + RD_{10} \]

Conclusion: If there are no interacting causal types in the population, the Additive Risk Difference is homogeneous (additive).

Key Takeaways for the Exam

Departure from Additivity: If you observe \(RD_{11} \neq RD_{01} + RD_{10}\),

you can infer that at least some causal interaction types are present in your population (assuming no confounding/bias).

Relative Scale Limitation: If you perform the same proof using Risk Ratios (RR),

you find that \(RR_{11} \neq RR_{10} \times RR_{01}\) even when there are no interacting types.

Implication: You cannot make strong statements about biological interaction based solely on the multiplicative scale.

Absence of Evidence: The absence of additive interaction (perfect additivity) does not guarantee the absence of causal types; it is possible for synergistic and antagonistic types to essentially “cancel each other out” in the population averages.
